# Dalya — Brand Foundations (Phase 1)

*Version 1.0 — strategic foundations, no visual system, no microcopy. Read this before reading anything else in /brand.*

---

## 0. How to read this document

This is a **strategy document**, not a style guide. It defines who Dalya is now, what Dalya is not, and the forcing-functions every future designer, engineer, copywriter, and hire is held to. Where Eric's brief was right, this document endorses it. Where the brief was wrong, this document says so and gives the alternative. The pushback in §6 is the most important section here. Read it twice.

## 1. Who Dalya is now

### The positioning shift

Dalya is no longer a consumer-facing brokerage marketing brand. Dalya is **the operating layer Dubai real estate agents work inside for hours every day**. The marketing surface still exists — it has to, agents and brokerages have to find us — but the marketing surface is downstream of the product surface now. The brand has to survive eight hours of agent use before it has to survive eight seconds of a homepage visit.

This is a real reorientation. A consumer-marketing brand sells trust in a single confident moment. A working-tool brand earns trust over the 1,247th screen you'll see this month, the one where you can't quite parse a column header and you've already been on three calls today. Those are not the same brand problem. The visual language, the typographic decisions, the tone of every empty state, the way errors get worded — all of it now serves continuous use, not first impression.

### Primary user

**A Dubai real estate agent, licensed under RERA, working a portfolio of off-plan resale and (likely) some primary inventory, managing roughly 15 to 80 active buyer conversations on any given week across WhatsApp, calls, and physical viewings.** They are not a desk worker. They are mobile-heavy, time-poor, multilingual (Arabic, English, often a third language), commission-incentivized, and chronically under-tooled. They are hiring Dalya to do the work that currently fragments across WhatsApp threads, Excel sheets, Property Finder dashboards, a brokerage CRM nobody loves, and the agent's own memory. They are hiring Dalya to be the single layer where buyer state, listing state, conversation state, and deal state are all observable in one place — and where Dalya's AI layer handles the conversations the agent can't be in at 11pm. **Not hiring Dalya** looks like: another quarter of losing buyers because someone didn't reply on a Friday afternoon, another quarter of guessing which lead is real, another quarter of working harder rather than working better.

### Secondary user

**The brokerage owner or sales manager** — usually managing 4 to 30 agents — who is hiring Dalya for visibility, accountability, and a defensible answer to "where are we on this deal?" They care less about the agent's day-to-day workflow and more about the dashboard layer: pipeline, conversion, agent performance, listing performance, where leads are stuck. They are hiring Dalya to replace the spreadsheet plus the weekly stand-up plus the gut feel. **Not hiring Dalya** looks like: continuing to run the brokerage by anecdote, continuing to miss revenue because no one can see it leaking, continuing to lose agents who left for brokerages with better infrastructure.

There is a third user the brief does not name and §6.5 argues we should add: **the operations / compliance person at the brokerage** — the one who handles Form A, Trakheesi, MOUs, listing authorizations, NOC tracking. Read §6.5 before signing off on a two-user model.

### The strategic shape

Dalya is **agent infrastructure**. Not an agent's assistant, not an agent's competitor, not an agent's marketing channel. Infrastructure. The brand should feel like the way Stripe feels to a finance team, the way Linear feels to an eng team, the way the well-designed building feels to the tenant: present when needed, invisible when not, and consistently more competent than the alternative.

## 2. Brand attributes

Three. Ordered. Each comes with a definition, an in-product translation, and a list of what the attribute **does not** authorize. The "does not" lists are doing more work than the definitions; they are the forcing functions when a future hire wants to make an aggressive design choice and reach for the brand book to justify it.

### Trustworthy

**Definition.** Factually grounded, regulator-aware, never overclaims, never improvises numbers, never pretends to be something it isn't. Trust here is the operational kind — "this tool didn't lie to me yesterday and probably won't lie to me today" — not the emotional kind.

**In product, this looks like.** Numbers that cite their source. AED figures rendered with deliberate, unmistakable typography so a misread is hard. Empty states that say "no data" rather than fabricating a chart. Error messages that say what went wrong and what to do. AI outputs that decline confidently when uncertain ("I don't have that on file") rather than guessing. RERA, DLD, and Trakheesi references in the surfaces where they actually matter (compliance, listing publication, agent profile), not as marketing wallpaper on every screen.

**Does NOT mean.** Stiff. Corporate. Legal-disclaimer-heavy. Risk-averse to the point of slowness. Decorated with badges and trust marks on every surface. Hand-holding. Anxious. The brand has to be trustworthy without being defensive — confident people don't oversell their credentials.

### Calm

**Definition.** Designed to be used for hours without visual or cognitive fatigue. Quiet by default. Loud only at the moments that warrant it (a deal closing, a regulatory deadline, a high-value lead escalating). Calm is the attribute most agents will notice subconsciously and most competitors fail at.

**In product, this looks like.** Restrained color (functional palette: status, semantics, hierarchy — not decorative). Generous whitespace where data density allows. No motion for motion's sake. No celebratory micro-interactions on routine actions. No notification fatigue — alerts are earned, not sprayed. Typography sized for sustained reading, not for screenshot-impact. Information hierarchy strong enough that the user's eye knows where to land without searching.

**Does NOT mean.** Boring. Empty. Slow-feeling. Sterile. White-on-white minimalism without character. Calm is not the absence of design — it's design that has been disciplined. A blank screen is not calm; a screen where every element earned its place is calm.

### Sharp

**Definition.** Fast, precise, dense where density helps, modern in a way that respects the user's intelligence. Sharp is the attribute that prevents Calm from sliding into bland. Sharp is what makes a 4pm power user feel that Dalya is keeping up with them, not slowing them down.

**In product, this looks like.** Tight information density on working surfaces (tables, queues, dashboards). Keyboard shortcuts that experienced users will find. Inline editing where it's faster than modal-and-save. Search and filter that return results before you've finished typing. Typography choices that make a column of AED figures legible at a glance. Loading states that feel like nothing is loading because nothing is slow. Copy that says the thing in five words instead of fifteen.

**Does NOT mean.** Aggressive. Loud. Brutalist. Bro-tech. Sharp is not "Linear taken too far" (Eric's phrase, correctly used). Sharp is competence rendered as speed and precision, not as visual hostility. A sharp tool feels good in the hand; it does not announce its own sharpness.

### Why this order

Trustworthy first because in this market (RERA-licensed real estate transactions involving lifetime-savings amounts of money on the agent's side, and seven-to-eight-figure transactions on the buyer's side), trust is the precondition for being used at all. Calm second because Calm is what makes a tool survive daily use; the most trustworthy tool that's visually exhausting gets abandoned for the worse but quieter one. Sharp third because Sharp without Trustworthy is a toy and Sharp without Calm is a stress machine.

**§6.4 argues this order may be wrong.** Read it before committing.

## 3. What Dalya is and is not

Twelve forcing-function pairs. When a future hire is making a judgment call, this is the table they consult.

1. **We are infrastructure, not a destination.** Users come to Dalya to do their work, not to admire Dalya. The brand experience is the work going well, not the brand showing up.

2. **We are confident, not assertive.** We state what we know plainly. We don't sell, persuade, or insist. Quiet voice, full information.

3. **We are Dubai-grounded, not Dubai-themed.** RERA, DLD, AED, Arabic-aware typography, prayer-time-aware notifications, weekend on Friday-Saturday. These are real. Skylines, palm trees, falcons, gold-leaf flourishes, "luxury" anything — these are theme. We do the first and refuse the second.

4. **We are a working tool, not a marketing surface.** The product is the brand. The website exists, but it is the entry point to the product, not the product itself.

5. **We are for professionals, not consumers.** Agents and brokerages. Not buyers. Not sellers. Not "all of Dubai real estate." Narrowing is a feature.

6. **We are an advisor's tool, not an advisor.** Dalya's AI layer supports the agent. The agent is the relationship. We never present Dalya as the closer; we present the agent as the closer with Dalya behind them.

7. **We are dense, not crowded.** Working surfaces show a lot, deliberately. Marketing surfaces show less, deliberately. The two surfaces have different rules.

8. **We are calm, not empty.** Whitespace is intentional. So is the absence of it on a queue view. Calm is rhythm, not vacancy.

9. **We are precise, not perfectionist.** Numbers are exact. Words are chosen. Animations are restrained. But: shipped beats unshipped, and we are not in the business of taste-driven paralysis.

10. **We are multilingual-by-default, not localized-later.** Arabic is a first-class citizen of the interface from day one. RTL is not a phase-three afterthought. Bilingual cognitive switching is the actual user reality.

11. **We are regulator-aware, not regulator-decorated.** RERA and Trakheesi appear where they matter (compliance, listing publication, audit). They do not appear as bottom-of-page trust badges on every screen. Trust through behavior, not through ornament.

12. **We are evolved, not rebranded.** The Dalya name, the wordmark, the Arabic identity, and the operating tone carry through. **The gold is a separate question — see §6.1.**

## 4. Voice positioning (Phase 1 only)

This is the high-altitude posture, not the microcopy rules. Detailed write rules are Phase 2.

### Register

**Direct, plain-language, present-tense, low-affect.** Dalya talks like a competent colleague briefing you on what happened: declarative, specific, no hedging-by-default, no warmth-as-performance, no enthusiasm-as-filler. Not cold — just unembellished. A useful mental model: the most senior agent in the brokerage, three years in, no longer impressed by themselves, briefing a junior on a deal. That voice.

The current chatbot voice in `BOT_RULES.md` is already close to this register and should be the anchor. The existing constraints — no "Great question!", no "Hope this helps!", no reflexive closing questions, no em-dashes that read AI-generated — survive the brand shift and probably extend to the rest of the product surface. The chatbot's voice is more developed than the rest of the brand's voice, and the rest of the brand should rise to meet it, not the other way around.

### Register-shifts by surface

The register holds across surfaces but the **pitch** of the voice modulates. Think of it as one personality speaking in different rooms.

- **Property-advisor chat output (buyer-facing).** Conversational but disciplined. Plain sentences. Real estate vocabulary used correctly. Cites numbers exactly. Declines confidently. This is the most developed voice today and the rest of the system inherits its constraints.

- **In-product copy for agents (the new center of gravity).** Tighter than chat. More telegraphic. Verbs over adjectives. "3 leads waiting" not "You have 3 new leads to attend to." Table headers, button labels, inline help, empty-state copy — all of this gets read hundreds of times per agent per week and earns its concision.

- **System messages and telemetry alerts.** Factual and consequence-first. "Offer above threshold. Forwarded to seller." "Listing publication blocked: Trakheesi required." Lead with what happened or what's blocking. The alert is not a sales pitch, not a celebration. If it warrants attention, the attention is in the placement, not in exclamation marks.

- **Marketing site / public-facing surfaces.** This is where the shift is largest. The current site is consumer-positioned (buyers and sellers landing on it). The new site is B2B-positioned (agents and brokerages evaluating). The voice gets slightly more declarative, slightly more confident about what Dalya is. Still no hype, no AI-forward language, no founder-letter overshare. Closer to Stripe's marketing voice than Intercom's. Names what the product does. Shows the product. Lets the product carry the argument.

- **Errors, validation, friction surfaces.** Plain, specific, action-oriented. Tell the user what went wrong, why, what to do next. No "Oops!" No anthropomorphism of the system. Never apologize on behalf of the software for things the user did.

### What we never say

- "Revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "AI-powered" used as adjective, "next-generation," "seamless," "frictionless," "world-class," "best-in-class."
- "Chatbot," "bot," "AI assistant" for Dalya's advisor layer. (Already locked in BOT_RULES.)
- "Hi there!", "Great question!", "Hope this helps!", "I'd be happy to help." (Already locked.)
- Emoji in product surfaces (already locked for chatbot; extends to product).
- Em-dashes that read AI-generated. (Already locked. The rule is preserved.)
- "Luxury," "premium," "opulent," "exclusive," "bespoke" — these belong to the brand Dalya is leaving, not the brand Dalya is becoming. Eric is right to reject them.

The full microcopy rule set is Phase 2. This section sets the frame.

## 5. What carries through, what changes

Eric proposed a carry-through list. Below is the audit: endorsed, modified, or challenged.

### Carry-through, endorsed

- **The Dalya name.** Endorsed. Distinctive, short, Arabic-rooted, already has equity in the property-advisor product. Keep.
- **The Arabic name treatment.** Endorsed and reinforced. The Arabic-name treatment is one of Dalya's actual differentiators in a market where most PropTech English-first-Arabic-as-afterthought. Treat it as core identity, not as localization. The Cultural Intelligence Strategist working in parallel will go deeper on this.
- **The wordmark.** Endorsed, with one note: the wordmark needs to be re-tested at the small sizes it will live at in a product chrome (app sidebar, browser tab, table-row brand mark). If it doesn't hold at 16px and 20px, the wordmark needs a refinement pass, not a redesign. Flag for the UI Designer.
- **The serious, regulator-aware tone of voice.** Endorsed. This is already the strongest part of the brand and should anchor everything else.
- **"Property advisor" terminology.** Endorsed and extended. Across the product, Dalya is never a "chatbot," "bot," or "AI assistant." Already canonical in BOT_RULES; now elevated to brand-level lock.

### Carry-through, challenged

- **The gold (#C9A96E) as brand-thread accent.** **Challenged.** See §6.1 for the full argument. The short version: gold as a "brand thread accent used sparingly" is a comfortable answer but probably the wrong one. Either gold has a real role or it should be retired. Don't keep it for sentimental reasons.

### Changes, endorsed

- **Light-default with parallel dark mode.** Endorsed. The current dark-only system was correct for a consumer-marketing brand projecting "Property intelligence. No pressure." It is wrong for a working tool used in a brightly-lit Dubai office or in a sunlit car between viewings. Most professional tools default light because most professional environments are bright. Dark mode is offered, not defaulted.

- **Typography moves from editorial display to legibility-optimized working weights.** Endorsed. The current "Plus Jakarta Sans / JetBrains Mono" pair is defensible at the working-weight level (Plus Jakarta is decent for UI; JetBrains Mono is correct for AED figures). The UI Designer should re-evaluate but this isn't a foregone change. Flag for them.

- **Surface system rebuilt for dense data.** Endorsed. The current "tonal surface layers, no borders for structure, ambient shadows" approach is a luxury-PropTech surface system, not a working-tool surface system. The new system needs hairline borders (yes, borders are back; structure beats elegance at density), strong row separation, predictable column rhythm. The UI Designer is leading this; foundations endorses the direction.

- **Color usage becomes functional.** Endorsed. Status colors (success, warning, error, info), semantic colors (offer state, NOC state, listing state, agent state), and brand colors are separate systems with separate jobs. The current system conflates "gold = brand = data emphasis = CTA = verified badge" — that's too many jobs for one color, and it's a structural reason gold has been overworked.

- **Component-first system with tokens and rules.** Endorsed. The current `CLAUDE.md` token reference is a starting point, not a design system. The Phase 2 deliverable is the actual system.

### Change not in the brief but recommended

- **"Property intelligence. No pressure." as a tagline.** Recommend retiring or repositioning. This tagline was written for buyers ("no pressure" is a reassurance to a consumer being sold to). It does not speak to agents and brokerages. An agent does not need to be told "no pressure" — they are the source of the pressure in the consumer-facing transaction. A new tagline, if we keep one at all, should be agent-facing and infrastructure-coded. Tagline rewrite is a Phase 2 deliverable but flagging the issue now so it doesn't carry through by inertia.

- **"Precise. Inviting. Modern."** as the three brand words. Recommend retiring. "Inviting" was a consumer word — it spoke to "come in, look around." It is the wrong word for a working tool. Trustworthy, Calm, Sharp are the new three. Old triplet retires.

## 6. Pushback section (required)

Eric asked for this section explicitly, and treating it as decoration would be a failure of the assignment. Five interrogations below. Three of them recommend changing Eric's stated position.

### 6.1 The gold carrying through

**Eric's position.** Gold survives, demoted from primary to brand-thread accent. Used for CTAs, key data emphasis, brand moments.

**The challenge.** A brand thread accent has to do real work to justify its existence. Otherwise it becomes vestigial — a color you can no longer fully justify but can no longer fully remove. We need to be honest about whether this is the case.

**The comparison Eric raised — Stripe's purple.** Stripe's purple is instructive but cuts the other way from the example Eric implied. Stripe's purple is not a "brand thread accent." It is a structural part of the Stripe interface: it codes the brand's primary action, it appears in the marketing system, it's strong enough that a Stripe screenshot is identifiable as Stripe from across a room. Stripe did not demote purple — they doubled down on it and built a discipline around it. Stripe's lesson is that a brand color survives demotion only when it is doing visible structural work. A purely sentimental brand color does not survive contact with a working tool.

**The other comparison — Intercom blue.** Intercom is the cautionary version. Intercom's bright blue served them well in the conversational-marketing era; it has been progressively neutralized in their B2B-enterprise pivot because it read too consumer and too excitable for the audience they wanted. The neutralization didn't kill the brand — it freed it. Intercom is more credible now without the brand-blue being a UI workhorse.

**Where this leaves the gold.** The gold (#C9A96E) was a luxury-PropTech signal. It worked in the consumer-marketing era because it coded "premium real estate" to buyers. It does not code "professional infrastructure" to agents. It codes the brand Dalya is leaving. Keeping it as a brand-thread accent risks doing exactly what Intercom got criticized for: signaling the prior era inside the new one.

**My recommendation.** Two options, with a strong preference between them.

- **Option A (preferred): retire the gold from the working surface entirely. Keep it only in the wordmark and one or two brand-moment surfaces (the login screen, the marketing site hero, a deal-closed celebration).** Inside the product, gold is gone. Status colors and semantic colors do the work that gold currently does (CTA color is a deliberate brand-neutral primary action color, not gold; data emphasis is typography and weight, not color; verified badges are an icon plus a sage tick, not a gold mark). This is honest: the brand has changed, the surface reflects the change, and the gold survives as wordmark heritage rather than as UI workhorse.

- **Option B (compromise): keep gold in one and only one product role — the AED figure typographic treatment for the primary asking price on a listing.** Nowhere else. Not CTAs, not badges, not active states, not focus rings. This is the most disciplined version of Eric's stated position, and it lives or dies by whether everyone on the team can hold the line on the single use.

I prefer A. Option B is the politically easier answer but is harder to enforce in practice and risks gold creeping back into a second and third role over the next two quarters. If we are evolving the brand, evolve it.

**The decision is Eric's**, but if Eric wants Option B he needs to write down the rule so explicitly that the next designer cannot rationalize a second use of gold. The brief as written does not yet do that.

### 6.2 The reference brand cluster

**Eric listed.** Linear, Notion, Stripe, Attio, Pipedrive, Intercom, Dropbox.

**The diagnosis.** This is a list of brands Eric admires, not a unifying thesis. Seven references is too many; some of them point in opposite directions; following all of them will produce a system that compromises every direction.

**Audit by reference:**

- **Linear.** Real fit. Density, restraint, dark-or-light dual mode, opinionated keyboard-first interactions, no-nonsense voice. Linear is probably the single closest reference for the working-surface DNA. **Keep.**

- **Stripe.** Real fit. Typographic hierarchy, brand confidence, the way they handle numbers and money, the way they make complex regulatory products feel approachable without being chirpy. The marketing-site posture Dalya should aim for is closer to Stripe than to anyone else on the list. **Keep.**

- **Notion.** Partial fit. The typographic clarity and whitespace discipline are useful references; the everything-is-a-document content model is irrelevant. **Use selectively** for typography reference; do not adopt the content model.

- **Attio.** Real fit, possibly the most directly applicable reference on the entire list. Attio is a CRM, B2B, dense-data, multi-pane workflow, the closest analog to what Dalya's working surface actually is. **Keep, and study it more than Eric implied.**

- **Pipedrive.** Reject. Pipedrive is a real CRM but the design system is dated, mid-2010s sales-tool aesthetic, pipeline-pipeline-pipeline visual fixation. Studying it would pull us toward a brand era Dalya should not occupy.

- **Intercom.** Partial fit on tone (approachability without losing credibility), wrong fit on visual system (too consumer-marketing-y, too bright-blue, too celebratory). **Use for tone reference only.** Do not look at Intercom for visual system cues.

- **Dropbox.** Reject. Dropbox's recent brand work (especially post-2017 brand refresh with the playful illustration system) points the opposite direction from where Dalya is going. Older Dropbox (clean, legible, blue-and-white) is a reasonable reference but those qualities are better-sourced from Stripe, which does them at higher fidelity.

**Recommendation.** Reduce the reference cluster to **three primary, two secondary**:

- **Primary (study deeply, return to repeatedly):** Linear (working-surface DNA), Attio (CRM pattern language), Stripe (typographic and marketing posture).
- **Secondary (consult for specific concerns):** Notion (typography and whitespace), Intercom (tone only — not visual).

Drop Pipedrive and Dropbox entirely. They will pull the system off-thesis.

### 6.3 "Evolution, not reset"

**Eric's position.** Evolution, not reset.

**The challenge.** The framing understates the rupture and risks holding back the design work that needs to happen.

**The honest version.** The brand identity (name, wordmark, Arabic treatment, voice DNA) is evolving. **The brand product is being replaced.** The consumer-facing marketplace brand and the agent-facing infrastructure brand are not the same brand wearing new clothes — they are two different brands with a shared name and a shared cultural posture. The wordmark and the Arabic treatment are continuity tokens. Everything downstream of them — the palette, the type system, the surface vocabulary, the IA, the tone register on most surfaces, the tagline, the brand triplet, the imagery system, the photography (if any), the iconography — is being rebuilt.

This matters for two reasons:

1. **It changes how aggressively the design work is allowed to depart from current Dalya.** "Evolution" implicitly tells the UI Designer to stay close to the current visual system. The honest framing — *identity continuity, product replacement* — gives the UI Designer permission to depart further from the current surface than they otherwise would. If we tell the team "evolution," we'll get a system that compromises with the old one. If we tell the team "the wordmark and name carry, everything else is rebuilt," we'll get a system that fits the new audience.

2. **It changes how we communicate the change externally.** "Evolution" suggests existing users (buyers, sellers, the people the current marketing brand was built for) should feel continuity. They are not the new audience. The new audience are agents and brokerages, most of whom have never seen the consumer brand. Communicating "evolution" to them is communicating about a brand they don't know. Communicating "this is Dalya, here is what it does for you" is the actual message.

**My recommendation.** Internally, drop "evolution" as the framing and use **"identity continuity, product replacement."** The wordmark, name, and Arabic treatment carry. The brand-as-experienced-by-the-user is being rebuilt around them. Externally, do not narrate the brand change at all. The new audience does not care that there was an old brand. Show them the product.

Eric is invited to push back on this — the "evolution" framing may be doing political work I can't see (with existing investors, with Mahoroba leadership, with previous design contributors). If so, keep "evolution" externally and use "identity continuity, product replacement" internally. But pick one and use it consistently.

### 6.4 The brand attribute priority order

**Eric's position.** Trustworthy > Calm > Sharp.

**The challenge.** I argued for this order in §2 and I believe it's defensible. But it deserves a second look, because the order is not obvious and the most plausible alternative ranks Calm above Trustworthy.

**The argument for Calm first.** A working tool that fails on Calm gets abandoned regardless of how trustworthy it is. Trustworthy is a precondition for adoption; Calm is the precondition for retention. In the agent infrastructure category specifically, agents will not give a daily-use tool more than two weeks to prove it isn't going to fatigue them. Most of Dalya's potential failure modes are Calm failures (overwhelm, noise, alert fatigue, ugly density, cramped screens), not Trustworthy failures.

**The counter-argument (the one I still hold).** In Dubai real estate, the trust threshold is the *entry threshold*. An agent will not let Dalya touch their book of business until they trust it; if Dalya is calm but not credibly trustworthy, the agent never reaches the surface long enough for Calm to matter. RERA, regulatory awareness, the AI-but-not-toy positioning, the data-integrity discipline in `BOT_RULES.md` — all of this is doing the Trustworthy work that gets us in the door. Calm is what keeps us there once in.

**My final position.** Trustworthy first, by a narrower margin than the brief implies. Calm and Sharp are nearly co-equal seconds. The danger is treating Trustworthy as a first-by-a-lot and then under-investing in Calm, which is the failure mode most likely to lose this product. Build for Trustworthy as the entry condition; budget design effort against Calm as the retention condition; let Sharp keep the system from sliding into either-or-blandness.

**Are these the right three at all?** I considered alternatives. **Useful** instead of one of these — rejected, because Useful is what the product is, not what the brand is. **Honest** as a fourth — rejected, because Honest collapses into Trustworthy. **Quiet** instead of Calm — considered, because "Quiet" is more specific, but Calm covers the cognitive-load dimension that Quiet doesn't and I think Calm is the more accurate descriptor. **Sharp** vs **Crisp** vs **Precise** — Sharp has more edge and prevents Calm from going soft; Precise is what we say about the product, not what we say about the brand personality. Sharp stays.

### 6.5 The user model

**Eric's position.** Primary user: Dubai real estate agent. Secondary user: brokerage owner.

**The challenge.** Both true, but the model is too thin to design against without more specification.

**The questions the brief does not answer:**

1. **Sole agent vs team agent.** A sole independent agent and an agent inside a 12-person brokerage have different daily workflows, different data-sharing rules, different escalation needs, different attitudes to dashboards. Are we designing for both? If so, what is the surface differentiation?

2. **Listing-side agent vs buyer-side agent vs dual-side.** A listing-side agent (handling sellers) lives in a different part of the product than a buyer-side agent (handling buyers and viewings). Dalya's current consumer-marketing brand had two user types (buyer, seller). The agent product probably has three or four (listing-side agent, buyer-side agent, brokerage admin, ops/compliance person). Which of these is the primary primary?

3. **The third user the brief omits.** The **operations / compliance person** at the brokerage — the one handling Form A, Trakheesi, NOC tracking, MOU drafting. In a typical Dubai brokerage of 10+ agents, this is a real role. The brief is silent on them, but the product almost certainly touches their work, and the brand will be evaluated by them when the brokerage signs up. They are not the buyer-facing user but they are an evaluator and a daily user.

4. **The user the agent serves.** Buyers (and to a lesser extent, sellers) still exist in the product surface — they're the people interacting with the property-advisor layer that the agent has deployed. They are not Dalya's customer anymore, but they're still in the product, and the buyer-facing surface is still Dalya's brand to the public. The brief does not address how the consumer-facing surface (buyer-visible chat, buyer-visible listing pages, the eventual buyer-side product) reconciles with the B2B brand. This is a real gap.

**My recommendation.** Before Phase 2, Eric should write one of:

- A three-user model: **buyer-side agent, listing-side agent (often the same person but with a different job to do), brokerage owner.** Operations/compliance person folded into "brokerage owner" as the second hat that role often wears.

- Or a four-user model that names ops/compliance separately.

And one paragraph each on the **buyer (still present in the product, no longer the customer)** and the **seller (still present in the product, no longer the customer)** — specifically, what role the brand plays in their experience of the product Dalya powers for the agent. This is not a tertiary concern; the buyer's experience of the property-advisor chat *is* the brand experience for the buyer, and they will form opinions about Dalya based on it.

Without this, the Phase 2 visual system risks being designed against an under-specified user, and we'll discover the gap when we ship to the first brokerage.

## 7. Strategic risks

Five risks that could quietly break this brand at scale. Listed because acknowledging them now is cheaper than discovering them in Q3.

### 7.1 The buyer-facing surface drifts from the agent-facing surface

Dalya powers the property-advisor chat that buyers interact with. The buyers do not know they are talking to an agent-deployed product; from their side it looks like a Dalya-branded conversation. If the buyer-facing surface stays in the old consumer-marketing aesthetic (dark, gold, "Property intelligence. No pressure.") and the agent-facing surface adopts the new B2B aesthetic, **Dalya becomes two visually unrelated brands sharing a wordmark**. This is survivable for a quarter and corrosive at scale. The brand system needs to define how the buyer-facing surface reconciles with the agent-facing one — probably as a quieter, lighter, more constrained variant of the same system, not as a separate brand. This is a Phase 2 deliverable but the risk is identified now.

### 7.2 Gold creeps back

Whether we choose Option A or Option B in §6.1, gold creeps back unless the rule is written hard and enforced ruthlessly for two quarters. Every new designer hired will reach for gold to "warm up" a CTA, a hero, a celebration screen. By Q4 it's back in five places. **The mitigation is a rule, not a guideline,** and a review checkpoint where a brand owner has veto on color additions.

### 7.3 The AI-forward temptation

Dalya is an AI-powered product in an industry (PropTech) currently saturated with AI marketing. There will be pressure — from investors, from competitors, from marketing instinct — to lean into "AI-powered" as a brand signal. The brief correctly rejects this. The risk is that the rejection survives Phase 1 and decays through Phase 3. **The mitigation is a brand-level lock that says "AI is the substrate, not the story,"** and an internal review whenever a marketing surface drafts AI-forward language. The chatbot rules in `BOT_RULES.md` already do this discipline at the conversation level; the brand level needs the same discipline.

### 7.4 The brand cannot scale to other markets without rupture

If Dalya succeeds in Dubai and the natural expansion path is Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, Doha, the brand currently — and the brand proposed here — is Dubai-coded. RERA, DLD, Trakheesi, the regulatory-aware tone, the specific Arabic treatment — these are Dubai. They are also probably right for Dubai. The risk is that the brand was built for Dubai and the founders later want it to be a Gulf-regional brand without rebuilding. **The mitigation is to surface this question explicitly before Phase 2.** If we are building a Dubai brand, name it; if we are building a regional brand with a Dubai-first surface, the IA decisions in Phase 2 need to anticipate the second market. This is a decision Eric needs to make, not a decision the brand team can make for him.

### 7.5 The brand becomes too quiet to be remembered

The brief correctly pushes away from luxury, premium, opulent, bold, vibrant, futuristic. The risk is over-correction — a brand so visually disciplined and so committed to "quiet confidence" that it has no signal of its own. A working tool that fades into the background can also fade out of the consideration set. **The mitigation is the wordmark and the Arabic treatment doing distinctive work** (those are the moments where the brand is allowed to be visibly itself), and the typography system having enough character (in chosen typeface, in numeric treatment, in micro-detail) that someone shown a Dalya screen with the wordmark cropped out can still recognize it. Phase 2 needs to test this explicitly: crop the wordmark, show three Dalya screens to a fresh viewer, ask them what they're looking at. If the answer is "some SaaS dashboard," the system is too quiet.
